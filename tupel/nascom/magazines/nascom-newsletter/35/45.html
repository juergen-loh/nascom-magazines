Design Considerations.

My own computer interests lie in the application of micropracessors to small design projects, often eaploying a 760 CPU plus minimal RAM and EPROM configurations. A single 2714 EPROM is often enough for the whole program, ie. 2048 (2K) bytes. A Nascom plus and EPROM programmer (such as the Gemini one) makes quite a fair development system. However, when testing a newly built piece of hardware (or a “target” system, as it le known) it can be necessary to repeatedly blow and erase EPROMS to find faults and debug the program. What is required is 2 chunk of memory which will Jook like an EPROM to the target system, but to which a host computer can read and write quickly. This would then replace the EPROM until program development is complete.

Several such emulators are commercially available, but tend to be expensive, as they incorporate their om’ aonitors, keyboards and displays. The device is controlled from a Z80 PIO, in this case, ports A and B of a Nascom 1, although, of course, other ports may be used.

As dram, the emulator plugs in directly in place of the single supply rail 2716 EPROM, although it could he extended to 2732 devices by adding extra RAM and decoding.
